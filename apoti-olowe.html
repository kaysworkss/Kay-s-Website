Artist statement

Àpótí Ọlọ́wẹ̀ is a love story rooted in Yoruba craft and memory. The series follows Ayo, a young woodcarver, and Morenike, his muse, as their relationship unfolds across six chapters,from a chiefs’ meeting that shapes their fate to an engagement and its aftermath.

Each chapter features a central digital painting paired with small digital sculptures: stools, charms, and carved objects that act as symbols and clues. These forms emulate the storytelling traditions of Yoruba artisans who used everyday objects to hold history, emotion, and meaning.

Inspired by Olowe of Ise and the broader tradition of Yoruba woodcarvers—many of whose names have been swallowed by history. These were artists who poured intention, culture, and history into carved wood. Their work endured; their names often did not, Àpótí Ọlọ́wẹ̀ is partly a reckoning with that erasure. The series asks what it means to carry a craft forward.

Though created digitally, Àpótí Ọlọ́wẹ̀ continues the philosophy that objects can speak. They can hold love, conflict, lineage, and the weight of tradition.

Ultimately, Àpótí Ọlọ́wẹ̀ is a tribute: to Yoruba storytelling, to the artistry that shaped generations, and to the evolving language of African digital expression.

Why crypto — and why this series?

Àpótí Ọlọ́wẹ̀ is a serialized digital art collection hosted on the Ethereum blockchain — a public, permanent ledger that records exactly who owns what. This is not simply a technical choice; it is a commitment to the work itself. When you collect a piece from this series, your ownership is written into the blockchain permanently. No platform can delete it, no middleman can dispute it, and no receipt can be lost. The artwork and your claim to it exist as long as the internet does.

Hosting the auction on-chain also means the token system is real and enforceable — when you hold five Entry tokens and burn them for a Bronze, that exchange happens transparently on the blockchain, not on a spreadsheet managed by anyone. When Gold tokens are airdropped to auction bidders, that is a verifiable, automated event. Every privilege in this series is built into the structure of the collection itself.

Collecting on-chain is also remarkably fast and frictionless. There are no lengthy bank transfers, no waiting days for a payment to clear, no forms to fill. Once your wallet is funded, a transaction settles on the Ethereum network in seconds — your token arrives, your ownership is confirmed, and you can move on.

As a new collector, entering through a serialized release like this is one of the best ways to start — the tiers are clear, the entry price is accessible, and you are directly supporting an artist doing something genuinely rare: weaving Yoruba cultural heritage, craft tradition, and serialized storytelling into a single cohesive body of digital work. The story, the research, and the intention behind every piece are documented. What you own will have context, and context is what gives art its lasting value.

The chapter auctions are also a genuine social experience. Bidding happens openly — you can see competing bids in real time, feel the momentum of a live auction, and engage with other collectors who are following the same story chapter by chapter. It is the energy of a gallery opening, without the geography. That shared experience, and the conversations it sparks, is part of what makes collecting this series worth doing.

Ready to get started? The five steps below walk you through everything — from setting up your wallet to placing your first bid.

01

Create a crypto wallet

A wallet is your personal account on the blockchain — it stores your tokens and lets you buy, sell, and receive digital art. The recommended wallet for collecting NFTs is Rabby Wallet, a free browser extension and mobile app known for its clean interface and built-in transaction safety checks. Download it, create an account, and you'll be given a secret recovery phrase — 12 words that are the master key to your wallet.

Keep it safe: Write your recovery phrase down on paper — in a notebook, a journal, anywhere physical you trust — and store it somewhere secure. Never share it with anyone, and never save it as a screenshot or in your notes app. Lose it and your wallet cannot be recovered.
02

Buy ETH to fund your wallet

The tokens in this series live on the Ethereum blockchain, so you'll need ETH (Ether) to pay for them. Purchase ETH through a regulated exchange — Coinbase and Kraken are beginner-friendly options available in most countries. You can also buy ETH directly inside Rabby Wallet using a card via its built-in on-ramp.

How much? Entry tokens are priced affordably. You'll also need a small extra amount (~$5–15 worth of ETH) to cover gas fees — the network's transaction cost, which varies by time of day.
03

Connect your wallet to OpenSea

OpenSea is the marketplace where Àpótí Ọlọ́wẹ̀ Entry Tokens are available. Visit opensea.io and click Connect Wallet in the top right — select Rabby Wallet and approve the connection in the pop-up. Your wallet is now linked and you're ready to collect.

Connecting is free. You are simply proving you own the wallet — no ETH is spent until you make a purchase.
04

Mint your Entry (Wood) Token

Click Mint Entry Token below. The OpenSea page will open — select the quantity you want and click Buy Now. Rabby Wallet will pop up showing the price and gas fee with a clear risk summary; review it, then confirm. Within moments the token will appear in your wallet and on your OpenSea profile.

Already a collector? If you hold five Entry (Wood) tokens you can burn them on Transient Labs to receive one Bronze token and unlock elevated collector privileges.
05

Participate in chapter auctions

With your wallet funded and your Entry Token held, you're eligible to bid on the artwork auctions that accompany each of the six chapters. Head to the auction page, connect your wallet, and place a bid. The highest bidder at closing time wins the piece — and receives a Gold Token airdropped to their wallet, with access to exclusive end-of-series rewards.

Research & work in progress

This is the thinking behind the work—opened up so you can walk through it. The references that shaped each decision, the questions still being turned over, the current state of the series. You are not just watching something finished; you are arriving while it is still alive. Updated as each chapter develops.

Foundation

Olowe of Ise — primary research

Deep study of Olowe of Ise and other Yoruba woodcarvers—their veranda posts, palace doors, vessels, and carved objects. Key references: the Òpó collection held at the Met, the lidded bowl held at the Smithsonian, and the Ikere palace doors now held at the British Museum. Focus on compositional innovations, symbolic language, and the oral praise poetry composed in their honour.

World-building

Developing Ayo’s world

Building the narrative architecture: who Ayo is, his craft, his relationship with Morenike, and the objects that populate his world. Each sculpture is a story object—part of a living Yoruba visual language, not decoration.

Cultural layer

Yoruba symbolic motifs

Researching carved stools, charms, ornamentation, and ritual objects as narrative devices. Studying how Yoruba craftwork embedded meaning inside form—and how to translate that into digital sculpture without losing it's charm and meaning.
